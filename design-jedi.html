01 — Context

Breaking into UX is harder than it should be.

Entry-level UX designers face a fragmented landscape: job listings scattered across a dozen platforms, no interview preparation resources tailored to the discipline, and portfolio feedback that only comes from peers who are equally lost.

Design Jedi was conceived as a single platform addressing all three pain points — a design-specific job board, AI-powered interview prep, and a mentorship network — built entirely from user research rather than assumptions.

02 — Research

Nine interviews before a single wireframe.

Remote interviews with 9 current UX design students and recent graduates revealed consistent patterns in how early-career designers navigate job searching. Participants were recruited from Pratt's graduate design programme and adjacent networks to ensure genuine early-career perspective.

The research ran alongside a comparative analysis of LinkedIn and Glassdoor — examining what general-purpose platforms do well and where design-specific needs fall through the cracks.

Interviews
9 remote interviews with current UX students and recent graduates. Focus on job-hunting behaviours, pain points, and current workarounds.
Comp. Analysis
Audit of LinkedIn and Glassdoor against design-specific needs — identifying gaps in interview prep, portfolio guidance, and community features.
Usability Testing
5 high-fidelity usability tests on the prototype. Each round surfaced a specific issue that informed the next iteration.

03 — Findings

Four pain points. One clear direction.

The interviews converged on four overlapping problems that general job platforms consistently fail to address for designers.

Pain Point 01
Interview engagement — candidates don't know what to expect in design interviews and have nowhere to practise portfolio presentation skills specifically.
Pain Point 02
Portfolio optimisation — without expert feedback, designers iterate on portfolios in the dark, unsure what hiring managers actually value.
Pain Point 03
Storytelling — translating research and design decisions into compelling case narratives is a learnable skill that has no structured learning path.

Peer networking emerged as an underserved need: participants wanted connections with people at the same career stage, not just senior mentors. The platform's community layer was designed around this insight.

04 — Design

Three pillars. One cohesive platform.

The design was organised around three core pillars that directly address the research findings: a design-specific job board aggregating listings across platforms, AI-powered interview preparation with video recording and feedback, and a mentorship and peer networking layer.

Job board wireframe · Design-specific filters · Aggregated listings from multiple sources

The information architecture was deliberately simple — three destinations from the home screen, with progressive disclosure for advanced features. Five rounds of usability testing shaped the navigation model, the onboarding flow, and the interview prep interface specifically.

High-fidelity prototype · Core screens · Job board, interview prep, and networking hub

A style guide and lightweight design system were produced alongside the prototype, covering typography, colour, component states, and interaction patterns — establishing a consistent visual language across the three pillars.

05 — Outcome

Intuitive architecture. Validated through testing.

Five usability test rounds across lo-fi and hi-fi fidelity produced an iterated prototype that participants could navigate without instruction. The core flows — job search, interview preparation, and peer connection — all tested cleanly in the final round.

The project established a repeatable research-to-design process that I've carried into every project since: structure insights before opening Figma, and test early enough that the findings can actually change the direction.
