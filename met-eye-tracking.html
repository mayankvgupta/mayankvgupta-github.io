01 — Context

Millions of visitors. One opaque interface.

The Metropolitan Museum of Art's website serves millions of virtual visitors per year, granting access to one of the world's largest art collections. Despite the scale of the audience, the interface had received limited evaluative research — leaving navigational gaps unaddressed.

This study used eye-tracking technology to go beyond what users say, capturing where attention actually landed — and where it didn't. The goal: generate evidence-based design recommendations the MET team could act on.

Study overview · Tobii Pro X2 · Metropolitan Museum of Art website

02 — Methodology

Eye tracking plus retrospective think-aloud.

Ten participants (7 graduates, 2 undergraduates, 1 unaffiliated) completed structured tasks on the MET website while eye movements were recorded using Tobii Pro X2 hardware and Tobii Pro Lab software. Sessions were followed immediately by a retrospective think-aloud (RTA) to surface conscious reasoning behind observed gaze patterns.

Each session concluded with a System Usability Scale (SUS) questionnaire, producing quantitative learnability and usability scores alongside qualitative data. The mixed-method approach was chosen specifically because eye tracking reveals attention without priming — participants can't easily lie to a heatmap.

Hardware
Tobii Pro X2 eye tracker + Tobii Pro Lab for data capture and heatmap generation.
Protocol
Structured tasks → eye tracking → retrospective think-aloud → SUS questionnaire.
Sample
10 participants across graduate, undergraduate, and unaffiliated backgrounds. Mixed prior experience with the MET site.

03 — Findings

The filters were there. Half the users never saw them.

The study returned a SUS score of 78.3 — learnability 81.3 and usability 77.5. Both sit in the "good" range, but the qualitative data revealed specific friction points the aggregate score obscures.

Finding 01
50% of participants missed the filter search functionality entirely — it existed, but never entered their field of attention.
Finding 02
Users follow an F-shaped reading pattern on filter sections. Horizontal filter arrangements are especially vulnerable to this scan path.
Finding 03
Long scroll on article pages causes visible fatigue and drop-off. One participant physically moved their head — a signal standard testing rarely captures.

"I barely registered 'Great Women Artists' even though it clearly says it there."

— Participant O, retrospective think-aloud

Gazeplots · Article page · Scroll-induced attention fatigue visible in downward drift of fixation points

The close-look article pages presented a separate problem: once inside an artwork article, there was no contextual navigation for lateral exploration. Users had to use the browser back button — interrupting the exploratory browsing the MET's collection was designed to enable.

"I moved my head here because I was tired of reading."

— Participant O, on scroll fatigue during long article
04 — Recommendations

Four targeted changes based on where attention went.

Rather than a broad redesign, the study produced four specific, evidence-grounded recommendations tied directly to observed gaze patterns and task failures.

  • Add in-page navigation to article pages — users shouldn't need the browser back button to explore the collection laterally.
  • Reorganise filters vertically with alphabetical anchor tags — the horizontal layout conflicts with the F-shaped scan pattern observed across all participants.
  • Add scroll affordances and reduce article page length — visible fatigue signals (head movement, long fixation gaps) indicate the format pushes past comfortable reading depth.
  • Expand filter categories — participants repeatedly tried filtering by attributes the current system doesn't support, generating silent dead-ends.

Filter reorganisation · Vertical layout with alphabetical anchors

Article page navigation · In-context lateral browsing

The client appreciated the depth and specificity of the recommendations — using eye-tracking data made each suggestion traceable to an observed behaviour rather than an assumption. Deliverables included a full research report, heatmap documentation, and annotated wireframes for each proposed change.
